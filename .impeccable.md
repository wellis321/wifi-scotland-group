## Design Context

### Users
Three audiences in equal measure:
- **General public in Scotland** — ordinary people who may lack reliable connectivity, arriving curious or frustrated, needing to feel seen and that action is possible
- **Activists & community organisers** — people already engaged in digital inclusion work, looking to connect with a campaign, share resources, and find others
- **Policymakers & journalists** — council staff, MSPs, reporters researching the issue who need credible evidence, cited sources, and a site they'd feel comfortable linking to from official documents

The common thread: everyone arrives with a degree of concern or intent. Nobody is browsing idly. The site must immediately signal that something real and serious is happening here.

### Brand Personality
**Three words**: Urgent. Credible. Rooted.

This is a rights issue, not a charity appeal. The campaign believes connectivity is infrastructure — like roads, water, heating. The voice is plain-spoken and direct, not academic. It respects the reader's intelligence. It doesn't plead; it argues.

Emotional goals: make visitors feel the issue is serious (not abstract), that the campaign is trustworthy (not fringe), and that joining or contacting is the obvious next step.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Editorial / magazine meets protest poster.** Think Positive Money or 38 Degrees — modern campaigning orgs with design confidence — crossed with the typographic authority of The Guardian or a quality Scottish broadsheet.

- **Ink-heavy** rather than pastel-light. The current cream palette is pleasant but too gentle for a rights campaign. Push toward higher contrast, more typographic mass on the page.
- **Strong display typography** — the headline IS the design. Big, confident, possibly condensed or slab-serif for punch. Body can stay clean and readable.
- **Editorial layout variety** — alternating rhythms, pull quotes, graphic bands, asymmetric sections. Break out of the uniform card grid.
- **Graphic identity moment** — one visual signature that people remember: a bold rule, a recurring geometric device, a strong colour band, a typographic treatment.
- Light mode primary (accessibility and credibility). The current warm paper tone can evolve but shouldn't go dark.

**References (what works about them):**
- **Positive Money**: Confident, modern, doesn't apologise for its politics. Uses type as a graphic element.
- **38 Degrees**: Immediate, action-oriented, visual hierarchy that makes the call to action feel inevitable.

**Anti-references (what to avoid):**
- Generic nonprofit charity aesthetic — soft images of sad people, muted greens, Comic Sans energy
- Corporate lobbying feel — overly polished, sterile, trust-by-committee
- The current AI slop palette: cyan on dark, purple-to-blue gradients, glowing accents
- Identical card grids with icon + heading + text repeated endlessly

### Design Principles
1. **The headline is the hero.** Typography carries the campaign's authority. Every page should open with a statement that makes you stop.
2. **Urgency without panic.** This is serious, not alarmist. Confident directness over breathless scrollbait.
3. **Serve three audiences without alienating any.** Structure and navigation should make the right path obvious whether you're a curious resident, an organiser, or a policy researcher.
4. **Earn credibility through specificity.** Real data, real places, real stories. Design should frame evidence so it lands — not hide it behind vague infographics.
5. **Every interaction should feel like forward motion.** Joining, contacting, reading more — the user should always know what to do next and feel pulled toward it.
